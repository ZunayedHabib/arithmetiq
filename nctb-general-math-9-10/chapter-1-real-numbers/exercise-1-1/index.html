    Question 1 of 12
    Question 1 🟢 Easy
    Which of the following is an irrational number? $$1)\ 0.\overline{3} \qquad 2)\ \sqrt{\dfrac{16}{9}} \qquad 3)\ \sqrt[3]{\dfrac{8}{27}} \qquad 4)\ \dfrac{5}{\sqrt{3}}$$
    📌 Strategy
    Try to simplify each option completely. If it reduces to a fraction \(\frac{p}{q}\) of two integers, it is rational. If it still contains a square root of a non-perfect-square number, it is irrational.

    Key idea: A rational number is any number that can be written as \(\frac{p}{q}\) where \(p\) and \(q\) are integers and \(q \neq 0\). Every repeating decimal and every terminating decimal is rational.
    Step 1 — Check option 1: \(0.\overline{3}\)
    A repeating decimal is always rational. We know from basic fractions:

    $$0.\overline{3} = 0.3333\ldots = \frac{1}{3}$$ This is a ratio of two integers — Rational ✗
    Step 2 — Check option 2: \(\sqrt{\dfrac{16}{9}}\)
    Both 16 and 9 are perfect squares (\(4^2 = 16\) and \(3^2 = 9\)), so the square root simplifies completely: $$\sqrt{\frac{16}{9}} = \frac{\sqrt{16}}{\sqrt{9}} = \frac{4}{3}$$ This is a ratio of two integers — Rational ✗
    Step 3 — Check option 3: \(\sqrt[3]{\dfrac{8}{27}}\)
    Both 8 and 27 are perfect cubes (\(2^3 = 8\) and \(3^3 = 27\)): $$\sqrt[3]{\frac{8}{27}} = \frac{\sqrt[3]{8}}{\sqrt[3]{27}} = \frac{2}{3}$$ This is a ratio of two integers — Rational ✗
    Step 4 — Check option 4: \(\dfrac{5}{\sqrt{3}}\)
    \(\sqrt{3}\) is irrational — 3 is not a perfect square, so it cannot be written as a fraction. Dividing a non-zero integer by an irrational number always gives an irrational result.

    We can rationalise the denominator to confirm: $$\frac{5}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{3}} \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{3}$$ Since \(\sqrt{3}\) is irrational, \(\dfrac{5\sqrt{3}}{3}\) is also irrational — Irrational ✓
    ✅ Final Answer
    Option 4) — \(\dfrac{5}{\sqrt{3}}\) is the irrational number.
    Question 2 🔴 Hard
    Prove that the following numbers are irrational:   1) \(\sqrt{5}\)   3) \(\sqrt{10}\)
    📖 Theory — Proof by Contradiction
    To prove a number is irrational, we use a method called proof by contradiction:

      1. Assume the opposite — suppose the number is rational.
      2. Follow the logic — work out what must be true under that assumption.
      3. Find a contradiction — show the assumption leads to something impossible.
      4. Conclude — since the assumption is false, the number must be irrational.

    We also use this critical fact about primes:
    If a prime \(p\) divides \(n^2\), then \(p\) must also divide \(n\).
    📌 Strategy
    Assume \(\sqrt{5} = \frac{p}{q}\) in lowest terms (meaning \(p\) and \(q\) share no common factor, i.e.\ \(\gcd(p,q) = 1\)). Square both sides. Use the prime divisibility rule to show both \(p\) and \(q\) must be divisible by 5 — a direct contradiction of "lowest terms".

    (1) Prove that \(\sqrt{5}\) is irrational

    Step 1 — Assume \(\sqrt{5}\) is rational
    Suppose \(\sqrt{5}\) is rational. Then we can write it as a fraction: $$\sqrt{5} = \frac{p}{q}$$ where \(p\) and \(q\) are integers, \(q \neq 0\), and \(\gcd(p,\, q) = 1\) (the fraction is fully reduced).
    Step 2 — Square both sides
    Squaring both sides of \(\sqrt{5} = \dfrac{p}{q}\): $$5 = \frac{p^2}{q^2} \implies p^2 = 5q^2 \quad \cdots (i)$$
    Step 3 — Show that 5 divides \(p\)
    From equation (i), \(p^2 = 5q^2\), so 5 divides \(p^2\).

    Since 5 is a prime number, if 5 divides \(p^2\) then 5 must also divide \(p\).

    So we can write \(p = 5m\) for some integer \(m\).
    Step 4 — Show that 5 also divides \(q\) — contradiction
    Substitute \(p = 5m\) into equation (i): $$(5m)^2 = 5q^2 \implies 25m^2 = 5q^2 \implies 5m^2 = q^2$$ So 5 divides \(q^2\), and since 5 is prime, 5 must also divide \(q\).

    We now have: 5 divides both \(p\) and \(q\).
    But this contradicts our assumption that \(\gcd(p,\, q) = 1\).

    $$\therefore\quad \sqrt{5} \text{ is irrational.} \qquad \blacksquare$$

    (3) Prove that \(\sqrt{10}\) is irrational

    📌 Strategy
    Same method — but this time we use the prime 2 (since \(10 = 2 \times 5\)) to derive the contradiction.
    Step 1 — Assume \(\sqrt{10}\) is rational
    Suppose \(\sqrt{10} = \dfrac{p}{q}\) where \(\gcd(p,\, q) = 1\). Squaring: $$p^2 = 10q^2 \quad \cdots (i)$$
    Step 2 — Show that 2 divides \(p\)
    From (i), \(p^2 = 10q^2\). Since \(10 = 2 \times 5\), clearly 2 divides \(p^2\).

    Since 2 is prime and 2 divides \(p^2\), we get 2 divides \(p\).

    Write \(p = 2m\).
    Step 3 — Show that 2 also divides \(q\) — contradiction
    Substitute \(p = 2m\) into (i): $$(2m)^2 = 10q^2 \implies 4m^2 = 10q^2 \implies 2m^2 = 5q^2$$ So 2 divides \(5q^2\). Since \(\gcd(2, 5) = 1\), 2 does not divide 5, so 2 must divide \(q^2\). Since 2 is prime, 2 divides \(q\).

    Both \(p\) and \(q\) are divisible by 2 — this contradicts \(\gcd(p,\, q) = 1\). $$\therefore\quad \sqrt{10} \text{ is irrational.} \qquad \blacksquare$$
    ✅ Final Answer
    Both \(\sqrt{5}\) and \(\sqrt{10}\) are irrational — proved by contradiction using the prime divisibility rule.
    Question 3 🟡 Medium
    (1) Find two irrational numbers between \(0.12\) and \(0.31\).

    (2) Find a rational and an irrational number between \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) and \(\sqrt{2}\).
    📖 Theory — Numbers Between Two Values
    Between any two distinct real numbers, there are infinitely many rational numbers and infinitely many irrational numbers. This is called the density of the real numbers.

    A practical way to find an irrational between \(a\) and \(b\): choose any non-perfect-square \(k\) with \(a^2 < k < b^2\). Then \(\sqrt{k}\) is irrational and lies in \((a,\, b)\).

    (1) Two irrational numbers between 0.12 and 0.31

    📌 Strategy
    We want \(\sqrt{k}\) with \(0.12 < \sqrt{k} < 0.31\). Squaring the inequality gives us the range for \(k\): square the boundaries and pick non-perfect-square values of \(k\) in between.
    Step 1 — Find the target range for \(k\)
    We want \(\sqrt{k}\) such that \(0.12 < \sqrt{k} < 0.31\). Squaring all three parts: $$0.12^2 \;<\; k \;<\; 0.31^2 \implies 0.0144 \;<\; k \;<\; 0.0961$$ Choose any non-perfect-square value of \(k\) inside this range.
    Step 2 — Choose two suitable values and verify
    Choose \(k = 0.05\) and \(k = 0.08\) (both lie inside \(0.0144\) to \(0.0961\)): $$\sqrt{0.05} = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{10} \approx 0.2236 \qquad \sqrt{0.08} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{5} \approx 0.2828$$ Verify both lie in the interval: $$0.12 \;<\; 0.2236 \;<\; 0.31 \;\checkmark \qquad 0.12 \;<\; 0.2828 \;<\; 0.31 \;\checkmark$$ Both are irrational because \(\sqrt{5}\) and \(\sqrt{2}\) are irrational, and dividing by a non-zero rational keeps them irrational.

    (2) A rational and an irrational number between \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) and \(\sqrt{2}\)

    📌 Strategy
    First find the decimal values of the boundaries. Then identify a simple integer or fraction (rational) and a simple surd (irrational) that both fall strictly between them.
    Step 1 — Find decimal values of the boundaries
    $$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$$ We need one rational and one irrational number strictly between \(0.707\) and \(1.414\).
    Step 2 — Find a rational number
    The integer \(1\) is rational, and: $$0.707 \;<\; 1 \;<\; 1.414 \;\checkmark$$
    Step 3 — Find an irrational number
    Consider \(\dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{2}\): $$\frac{\sqrt{6}}{2} \approx \frac{2.449}{2} \approx 1.225$$ $$0.707 \;<\; 1.225 \;<\; 1.414 \;\checkmark$$ \(\dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{2}\) is irrational because \(\sqrt{6}\) is irrational (6 is not a perfect square), and dividing by 2 does not make it rational.
    ✅ Final Answer
    (1) Two irrational numbers between 0.12 and 0.31:  \(\dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{10} \approx 0.2236\)  and  \(\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{5} \approx 0.2828\)

    (2) Between \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) and \(\sqrt{2}\):   Rational: \(\mathbf{1}\)  ·  Irrational: \(\dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{2}\)
    Question 4 🟢 Easy
    Convert the following into repeating decimal fractions:   1) \(\dfrac{1}{6}\)   2) \(\dfrac{7}{11}\)   4) \(3\dfrac{8}{15}\)
    📖 Theory — Fractions as Recurring Decimals
    Every fraction \(\frac{p}{q}\) (where \(q\) has prime factors other than 2 and 5) produces a recurring decimal — a decimal where a block of digits repeats forever. The repeating block is called the recurring period.

    To find it: perform long division. Whenever a remainder repeats, the decimal digits from that point cycle forever. We write the repeating block with an overline: \(0.\overline{63}\) means \(0.636363\ldots\)
    📌 Strategy
    Perform long division step by step. Record each remainder. As soon as a remainder appears for the second time, the decimal has started to repeat — stop and write the overline notation.
    Step 1 — Convert \(\dfrac{1}{6}\)
    Divide 1 by 6 using long division:

        \(10 \div 6 = 1\) remainder \(4\)
        \(40 \div 6 = 6\) remainder \(4\)  ← remainder repeats

    The digit 6 repeats forever. The 1 before it is non-recurring: $$\frac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6}$$
    Step 2 — Convert \(\dfrac{7}{11}\)
    Divide 7 by 11:

        \(70 \div 11 = 6\) remainder \(4\)
        \(40 \div 11 = 3\) remainder \(7\)
        \(70 \div 11 = 6\) remainder \(4\)  ← cycle repeats

    The block "63" repeats from the very start: $$\frac{7}{11} = 0.\overline{63}$$
    Step 3 — Convert \(3\dfrac{8}{15}\)
    The whole number 3 stays as is. Convert the fractional part \(\dfrac{8}{15}\):

        \(80 \div 15 = 5\) remainder \(5\)
        \(50 \div 15 = 3\) remainder \(5\)  ← remainder repeats

    So \(\dfrac{8}{15} = 0.5\overline{3}\), and therefore: $$3\frac{8}{15} = 3.5\overline{3}$$
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$\frac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6} \qquad \frac{7}{11} = 0.\overline{63} \qquad 3\frac{8}{15} = 3.5\overline{3}$$
    Question 5 🟡 Medium
    Convert the following into simple fractions:   1) \(0.\overline{2}\)   2) \(0.\overline{35}\)   4) \(3.\overline{78}\)
    📖 Theory — The Algebraic Cancellation Method
    The key insight: let \(x\) equal the recurring decimal. Multiply both sides by \(10^n\) where \(n\) is the number of digits in the repeating block. This shifts the decimal so the recurring part lines up exactly with the original. Subtracting eliminates the infinite tail — leaving a simple equation to solve.
    📌 Strategy
    Formula — shortcut for pure recurring decimals:
    $$0.\overline{d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n} = \frac{\text{the recurring block}}{\underbrace{99\cdots9}_{n \text{ nines}}}$$ For a decimal with a whole number part (like \(3.\overline{78}\)), apply the algebraic method to the full number — the whole number part cancels naturally in the subtraction.
    Step 1 — Convert \(0.\overline{2} = 0.2222\ldots\)
    Let \(x = 0.2222\ldots\)

    Multiply by 10 (the recurring block has 1 digit): $$10x = 2.2222\ldots$$ Subtract the original equation \(x = 0.2222\ldots\): $$10x - x = 2.2222\ldots - 0.2222\ldots$$ $$9x = 2 \implies x = \frac{2}{9}$$
    Step 2 — Convert \(0.\overline{35} = 0.353535\ldots\)
    Let \(x = 0.353535\ldots\)

    Multiply by 100 (the recurring block has 2 digits): $$100x = 35.353535\ldots$$ Subtract: \(100x - x = 35.353535\ldots - 0.353535\ldots\) $$99x = 35 \implies x = \frac{35}{99}$$
    Step 3 — Convert \(3.\overline{78} = 3.787878\ldots\)
    Let \(x = 3.787878\ldots\)

    Multiply by 100 (recurring block has 2 digits): $$100x = 378.787878\ldots$$ Subtract: \(100x - x = 378.787878\ldots - 3.787878\ldots\) $$99x = 375 \implies x = \frac{375}{99}$$ Simplify: \(\gcd(375, 99) = 3\), so: $$x = \frac{125}{33}$$
    📝 Note
    Always check for common factors after the subtraction step. Here \(375 = 3 \times 125\) and \(99 = 3 \times 33\) — both divisible by 3. Dividing gives the fully simplified fraction \(\frac{125}{33}\).
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$0.\overline{2} = \frac{2}{9} \qquad 0.\overline{35} = \frac{35}{99} \qquad 3.\overline{78} = \frac{125}{33}$$
    Question 6 🟡 Medium
    Express as similar repeating decimal fractions:   1) \(2.\overline{23}\) and \(5.\overline{235}\)
    📖 Theory — Similar Recurring Decimal Fractions
    Two recurring decimals are called similar when they have:
      • the same number of non-recurring digits after the decimal point, and
      • the same length of recurring period.

    Similar decimals are easier to add and subtract because the recurring blocks line up. To make two decimals similar, we extend each recurring block by repeating it until both reach a common period length.
    📌 Strategy
    Step: Find the LCM of the two period lengths. Repeat each recurring block \(\frac{\text{LCM}}{\text{period}}\) times to extend it to the common length.

    For example, a period-2 block repeated 3 times gives period 6.
    Step 1 — Identify the period of each decimal
    \(2.\overline{23}\): the block "23" repeats  →  period = 2

    \(5.\overline{235}\): the block "235" repeats  →  period = 3

    Both have zero non-recurring digits (the recurring part starts immediately after the decimal point). Good — no alignment needed there.
    Step 2 — Find the LCM of the two periods
    $$\text{LCM}(2,\; 3) = 6$$ Both decimals must be rewritten with a recurring period of length 6.
    Step 3 — Extend each recurring block to length 6
    For \(2.\overline{23}\): period 2 → repeat "23" three times → "232323" $$2.\overline{23} = 2.\overline{232323}$$ For \(5.\overline{235}\): period 3 → repeat "235" twice → "235235" $$5.\overline{235} = 5.\overline{235235}$$ Both now have period 6 and zero non-recurring digits — they are similar.
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$2.\overline{23} = 2.\overline{232323} \qquad 5.\overline{235} = 5.\overline{235235}$$
    Question 7 🟡 Medium
    Add:   \(0.\overline{45} + 0.\overline{134}\)
    📌 Strategy
    Convert each recurring decimal to a fraction using the shortcut: recurring block ÷ matching number of 9s. Then add the fractions using a common denominator.
    Step 1 — Convert \(0.\overline{45}\) to a fraction
    Two recurring digits → divide by 99: $$0.\overline{45} = \frac{45}{99}$$ Simplify: \(\gcd(45, 99) = 9\), so: $$\frac{45}{99} = \frac{5}{11}$$
    Step 2 — Convert \(0.\overline{134}\) to a fraction
    Three recurring digits → divide by 999: $$0.\overline{134} = \frac{134}{999}$$ \(\gcd(134, 999) = 1\), so this is already fully simplified.
    Step 3 — Find the LCM of the denominators
    We need a common denominator for \(\frac{5}{11}\) and \(\frac{134}{999}\).

    \(999 = 3^3 \times 37\). Since 11 is prime and does not divide 999: $$\text{LCM}(11,\; 999) = 11 \times 999 = 10989$$
    Step 4 — Add the fractions
    $$\frac{5}{11} + \frac{134}{999} = \frac{5 \times 999}{10989} + \frac{134 \times 11}{10989} = \frac{4995 + 1474}{10989} = \frac{6469}{10989}$$ \(\gcd(6469, 10989) = 1\), so this fraction is fully simplified.
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$0.\overline{45} + 0.\overline{134} = \frac{6469}{10989}$$
    Question 8 🟡 Medium
    Subtract:   1) \(3.\overline{4} - 2.\overline{13}\)    3) \(8.\overline{49} - 5.\overline{356}\)
    📌 Strategy
    For each number, let \(x\) equal the full decimal (including the whole number part) and apply the algebraic method to convert it to a fraction. Then subtract using a common denominator.

    (1)  \(3.\overline{4} - 2.\overline{13}\)

    Step 1 — Convert \(3.\overline{4}\) and \(2.\overline{13}\) to fractions
    For \(3.\overline{4} = 3.4444\ldots\): let \(x = 3.4444\ldots\), then \(10x = 34.4444\ldots\), subtract: \(9x = 31\) $$3.\overline{4} = \frac{31}{9}$$ For \(2.\overline{13} = 2.1313\ldots\): let \(x = 2.1313\ldots\), then \(100x = 213.1313\ldots\), subtract: \(99x = 211\) $$2.\overline{13} = \frac{211}{99}$$
    Step 2 — Subtract
    Convert \(\dfrac{31}{9}\) to ninety-ninths: \(\dfrac{31}{9} = \dfrac{341}{99}\) $$\frac{341}{99} - \frac{211}{99} = \frac{130}{99}$$ Converting back: \(\dfrac{130}{99} = 1.\overline{31}\)

    Check: \(3.4444\ldots - 2.1313\ldots = 1.3131\ldots\; \checkmark\)

    (3)  \(8.\overline{49} - 5.\overline{356}\)

    Step 1 — Convert both decimals to fractions
    For \(8.\overline{49} = 8.4949\ldots\): let \(x = 8.4949\ldots\), then \(100x = 849.4949\ldots\), subtract: \(99x = 841\) $$8.\overline{49} = \frac{841}{99}$$ For \(5.\overline{356} = 5.356356\ldots\): let \(x = 5.356356\ldots\), then \(1000x = 5356.356356\ldots\), subtract: \(999x = 5351\) $$5.\overline{356} = \frac{5351}{999}$$
    Step 2 — Find the LCM of 99 and 999
    $$99 = 3^2 \times 11 \qquad 999 = 3^3 \times 37$$ $$\text{LCM}(99,\; 999) = 3^3 \times 11 \times 37 = 10989$$
    Step 3 — Subtract
    $$\frac{841}{99} - \frac{5351}{999} = \frac{841 \times 111}{10989} - \frac{5351 \times 11}{10989} = \frac{93351 - 58861}{10989} = \frac{34490}{10989}$$ \(\gcd(34490, 10989) = 1\), so this is fully simplified.
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$3.\overline{4} - 2.\overline{13} = \frac{130}{99} = 1.\overline{31}$$ $$8.\overline{49} - 5.\overline{356} = \frac{34490}{10989}$$
    Question 9 🟡 Medium
    Multiply:   1) \(0.\overline{3} \times 0.\overline{6}\)    3) \(0.\overline{62} \times 0.\overline{3}\)
    📌 Strategy
    Convert each recurring decimal to a fraction using the shortcut (recurring block ÷ matching 9s). Multiply the fractions. Simplify fully, and check whether the result is a recognisable recurring decimal.

    (1)  \(0.\overline{3} \times 0.\overline{6}\)

    Step 1 — Convert to fractions and multiply
    $$0.\overline{3} = \frac{3}{9} = \frac{1}{3} \qquad 0.\overline{6} = \frac{6}{9} = \frac{2}{3}$$ $$\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{2}{9} = 0.\overline{2}$$

    (3)  \(0.\overline{62} \times 0.\overline{3}\)

    Step 1 — Convert to fractions and multiply
    Two recurring digits → divide by 99: $$0.\overline{62} = \frac{62}{99} \qquad 0.\overline{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$ $$\frac{62}{99} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{62}{297}$$ Check for common factors: \(62 = 2 \times 31\) and \(297 = 3^3 \times 11\) share none, so this is fully simplified.
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$0.\overline{3} \times 0.\overline{6} = \frac{2}{9} = 0.\overline{2}$$ $$0.\overline{62} \times 0.\overline{3} = \frac{62}{297}$$
    Question 10 🟡 Medium
    Divide:   1) \(0.\overline{3} \div 0.\overline{6}\)    3) \(2.\overline{37} \div 0.\overline{45}\)
    📌 Strategy
    Convert to fractions. Then divide by multiplying by the reciprocal: \(\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}\). Look for common factors between numerator and denominator and cancel them before multiplying.

    (1)  \(0.\overline{3} \div 0.\overline{6}\)

    Step 1 — Convert to fractions and divide
    $$0.\overline{3} = \frac{1}{3} \qquad 0.\overline{6} = \frac{2}{3}$$ $$\frac{1}{3} \div \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$

    (3)  \(2.\overline{37} \div 0.\overline{45}\)

    Step 1 — Convert \(2.\overline{37}\) to a fraction
    Let \(x = 2.3737\ldots\), then \(100x = 237.3737\ldots\), subtract: \(99x = 235\) $$2.\overline{37} = \frac{235}{99}$$
    Step 2 — Convert \(0.\overline{45}\) to a fraction
    $$0.\overline{45} = \frac{45}{99} = \frac{5}{11}$$
    Step 3 — Divide and simplify
    $$\frac{235}{99} \div \frac{5}{11} = \frac{235}{99} \times \frac{11}{5} = \frac{235 \times 11}{99 \times 5}$$ Factor to cancel: \(235 = 5 \times 47\) and \(99 = 9 \times 11\): $$= \frac{\cancel{5} \times 47 \times \cancel{11}}{9 \times \cancel{11} \times \cancel{5}} = \frac{47}{9} = 5.\overline{2}$$
    ✅ Final Answer
    $$0.\overline{3} \div 0.\overline{6} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$ $$2.\overline{37} \div 0.\overline{45} = \frac{47}{9} = 5.\overline{2}$$
    Question 11 🟡 Medium
    Write down the exact value and approximate value to 4 decimal places:   2) \(0.\overline{25}\)   3) \(1.\overline{34}\)
    📌 Strategy
    Convert the recurring decimal to a fraction — that is its exact value. Then write out the decimal expansion and apply the rounding rule:

    Look at the 5th decimal digit. If it is 5 or more, round the 4th digit up. If it is less than 5, leave the 4th digit unchanged (round down).

    (2)  \(0.\overline{25}\)

    Step 1 — Find the exact value
    Two recurring digits → divide by 99: $$0.\overline{25} = \frac{25}{99}$$ Decimal expansion: \(0.25252525\ldots\)
    Step 2 — Approximate to 4 decimal places
    The first five decimal places: \(0.2525\mathbf{2}\ldots\)

    The 5th digit is 2 (less than 5) → round down (keep the 4th digit as is): $$0.\overline{25} \approx 0.2525$$

    (3)  \(1.\overline{34}\)

    Step 1 — Find the exact value
    $$1.\overline{34} = 1 + \frac{34}{99} = \frac{99 + 34}{99} = \frac{133}{99}$$ Decimal expansion: \(1.34343434\ldots\)
    Step 2 — Approximate to 4 decimal places
    The first five decimal places: \(1.3434\mathbf{3}\ldots\)

    The 5th digit is 3 (less than 5) → round down: $$1.\overline{34} \approx 1.3434$$
    ✅ Final Answer
    NumberExact valueApprox (4 d.p.)
    \(0.\overline{25}\)\(\dfrac{25}{99}\)\(0.2525\)
    \(1.\overline{34}\)\(\dfrac{133}{99}\)\(1.3434\)
    Question 12 🟢 Easy
    State whether each of the following is rational or irrational: $$\mathbf{2)}\; \sqrt{9} \qquad \mathbf{3)}\; \sqrt{11} \qquad \mathbf{5)}\; \frac{\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{7}} \qquad \mathbf{6)}\; \frac{\sqrt{27}}{\sqrt{48}}$$
    📌 Strategy
    Simplify each expression as far as possible. If the result is a plain integer or a fraction of two integers, it is rational. If it still contains a square root of a number that is not a perfect square, it is irrational.

    Important: never assume a ratio of two surds is irrational — always simplify first.
    Step 1 — Classify \(\sqrt{9}\)
    $$\sqrt{9} = 3$$ A whole number is rational.  →  Rational
    Step 2 — Classify \(\sqrt{11}\)
    Is 11 a perfect square? We know \(3^2 = 9\) and \(4^2 = 16\) — there is no integer whose square equals 11. Its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating.

     →  Irrational
    Step 3 — Classify \(\dfrac{\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{7}}\)
    $$\frac{\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{7}} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{7}} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{7}} = \frac{2\sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{7}}{7} = \frac{2\sqrt{14}}{7}$$ Since 14 is not a perfect square, \(\sqrt{14}\) is irrational, and so is \(\dfrac{2\sqrt{14}}{7}\).

     →  Irrational
    Step 4 — Classify \(\dfrac{\sqrt{27}}{\sqrt{48}}\)
    $$\frac{\sqrt{27}}{\sqrt{48}} = \sqrt{\frac{27}{48}}$$ Simplify the fraction inside: \(\dfrac{27}{48} = \dfrac{9}{16}\) (divide both by 3). $$\sqrt{\frac{9}{16}} = \frac{\sqrt{9}}{\sqrt{16}} = \frac{3}{4}$$ Since both 9 and 16 are perfect squares, the result is a fraction of integers.

     →  Rational
    📝 Note
    \(\dfrac{\sqrt{27}}{\sqrt{48}}\) looks like it should be irrational — but after simplifying \(\frac{27}{48} = \frac{9}{16}\), both numerator and denominator are perfect squares, so the surds cancel out completely and give a rational answer \(\frac{3}{4}\). Always simplify before concluding.
    ✅ Final Answer

    \(\dfrac{\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{7}}\)\(\dfrac{2\sqrt{14}}{7}\)Irrational
